I graduated from the University of Uyo Faculty of Law in 2025. My dissertation looked at Nigeria's anti-corruption legal framework and asked a question I still have not stopped asking: why does enforcement fail so consistently when the legislation says otherwise?

Most of my research sits at the intersection of anti-corruption institutional design, UNCAC implementation, and whistleblower protection. I am particularly interested in how the African human rights system handles people who report wrongdoing. The short answer is: not well. The longer answer is what I keep publishing about.

I also write fiction and personal essays. I think the best governance writing and the best storytelling do the same thing: they make you feel the weight of something you could previously walk past without noticing. That is what I am trying to do in both forms.

I interned at the Akwa Ibom State Ministry of Justice during my studies, and at Nwoko and Co, Access Law House, and Humanity Voice Foundation. I am open to research collaboration, legal commentary, policy writing, and editorial commissions.

TheCable   May 28, 2026

Government Built a Whistleblowing Portal But Forgot to Enact Laws to Make Whistleblowing Safe

Nigeria's federal whistleblowing policy has no statutory force. You cannot enforce it in any Nigerian court. This piece follows Yisa Usman, a former civil servant who used the government's own reporting channel, then spent the next year losing his job, facing criminal charges, and having police sent to his home. At no point did Nigerian law require anyone to stop and ask whether what was happening to him was legal. Because there is no such law.

When the Extraterritorial Enforcer Steps Back: West Africa

What happens to anti-corruption enforcement in West Africa when external pressure that historically drove compliance withdraws? Which regional institutions step into that space? This piece, published in the University of Durham's Global Policy Journal, asks what happens when the answer to both questions turns out to be: not much.

International Law Blog   June 8, 2026

Legislative Omission as Treaty Breach: Nigeria, the AUCPCC, and What the ECOWAS Court Has Left Undone

Nigeria ratified a convention requiring statutory whistleblower protection in 2006. Three separate bills have died in committee since 2001. This piece argues that two decades of consistent legislative inaction has crossed from a domestic policy failure into a breach of a binding treaty obligation, and asks what the ECOWAS Court of Justice should have done about it by now.

UNCAC Article 33 and the Absent Obligation: Nigeria's Whistleblower Protection Gap as an International Problem

UNCAC Article 33 only asks states to consider whistleblower protection, never to enact it. This piece, published on the international law platform IntLawGrrls, argues that the gap left by that soft language is not gender neutral. Corruption falls harder on people excluded from the informal networks where protection gets quietly negotiated, and those networks are predominantly male.

Research Interests

Where my thinking lives.

01

Anti-Corruption Institutional Design

The EFCC and ICPC were built on specific structural assumptions about Nigerian politics. Most of those assumptions have not survived. I am interested in the gap between the framework on paper and the institution in practice, and in what a redesigned architecture would actually need to look like to close it.

02

Whistleblower Protection

Nigeria ratified the African Union anti-corruption convention in 2006. The convention requires member states to protect informants through legislation. That law has not been passed after nearly twenty years. I research why it keeps dying in committee, and what the African human rights system could do about it if it chose to act.

03

Asset Recovery

The legal pathways for recovering stolen public funds exist on paper. Getting them to work requires navigating POCA, UNCAC, and international cooperation frameworks that each have quiet places where they tend to close. That is where I focus.

04

Governance Transparency and Civic Accountability

Campaign finance non-enforcement. The architecture of public accountability in West Africa. How information moves between institutions and the people those institutions are supposed to serve, and what happens when it does not move at all.
