I graduated from the University of Uyo Faculty of Law in 2025. My undergraduate dissertation looked at Nigeria's anti-corruption legal framework, specifically the distance between what the legislation promises and what the institutions actually deliver. That question is still the one I find most interesting.

My research sits at the intersection of anti-corruption law, UNCAC implementation, asset recovery under POCA, and whistleblower protection. I am particularly focused on how the African human rights system handles the protection of people who report wrongdoing. The honest answer is: not well. That gap is what I keep returning to.

I also write fiction and essays. The best governance writing and the best storytelling share one thing in common: they make you feel the weight of something you could previously walk past. That is what I am after, in both modes.

I interned at the Akwa Ibom State Ministry of Justice during my studies, and at Nwoko and Co, Access Law House, and Humanity Voice Foundation. I am open to research collaboration, legal commentary, policy writing, and editorial commissions.

TheCable · May 28, 2026

Government Built a Whistleblowing Portal But Forgot to Enact Laws to Make Whistleblowing Safe

Nigeria's federal whistleblowing policy has no statutory force. You cannot enforce it in court. This piece tells the story of Yisa Usman, a man who used the government's own reporting channel, got dismissed, faced criminal charges, and had police sent to his home. The law never required anyone to stop and ask whether any of that was legal. Because there is no such law.

International Law Blog · June 8, 2026

Legislative Omission as Treaty Breach: Nigeria, the AUCPCC, and What the ECOWAS Court Has Left Undone

Nigeria ratified a convention requiring whistleblower protection in 2006. Three protection bills have died in committee since 2001. This piece argues that twenty years of deliberate legislative inaction has crossed from domestic policy failure into a breach of a binding treaty obligation, and asks what the ECOWAS Court should have done about it.
